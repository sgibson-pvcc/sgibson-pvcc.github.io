Electoral College


Elecrtoral College Definition:

it is a body of people representing the US, who formally cast votes for the election of the president and vice president.

What is it?
The Electoral College is the people who, on the voters’ behalf, cast votes for the president and Vice President of the United States. For each house representative and Senator there is a person to vote for the people of the country

• How does it work?
All electoral votes go to the candidate who receives the most votes in that state. For example, say Mickey Mouse was running against Garfield (the cat), the electoral representative would vote for whoever had the most votes in their district. Mickey Mouse had 90% of the vote in the district, the Electoral Representative would cast their vote for Mickey.

• When did it start?
In 1787, the Electoral College was added to the U.S. Constitution, the Electoral College is the formal body which elects the President and Vice President of the United States.

• Why it was started?
The electoral college was started as a different way to elect the president by popular vote or by Congress.


• How are the people chosen to be in the Electoral College?
Electors could be state elected officials, state party leaders, or people in the state that have a personal or political business with the partys' presidential choice


• How many “electors” are there in the college and how many votes are needed to win?
There are 538 electors in the electoral college. To win, a presidential candidate needs 270 votes to win the election


• What Happens if the candidates do not get the majority of the 538 votes?
The election for President is decided in the House of Representatives, with each state representative getting only one vote.
